# <a id="_Toc224818287"></a>The Strategy: Lower Risk, Higher Optionality

This roadmap is built around one principle: compound your assets without burning your bridges\. Oracle provides the W\-2 income, benefits, and institutional credibility\. FAU provides the academic affiliation, teaching fulfillment, and AACSB SA status\. The consulting LLC captures the upside of your rare expertise\. Publications build a permanent, portable reputation\. Certifications signal ongoing technical currency\. Each component reinforces the others\.

If Oracle stays solid, you have a thriving portfolio career\. If a layoff happens, you’ve already built the runway—a brand, a client pipeline, a publication record, and credentials that no single employer can take away\.

## <a id="_Toc224818288"></a>Your Unique Expertise: Cruise Line Shipboard IT Operations

Your consulting niche is not generic hotel cloud migration\. It is cruise line shipboard IT operations—specifically, providing consulting services to cruise ship operators using Oracle Hospitality Cruise Shipboard Property Management System \(SPMS\)\. This is an on\-premises system, not cloud, and it is the central data hub for virtually every aspect of hospitality operations aboard a vessel:

- Guest and crew profile management, onboard identification cards \(magnetic stripe, barcode, RFID\), and gangway security
- Mobile mustering and safety compliance \(muster lists, safety drill scheduling, ILO violation tracking\)
- Point\-of\-sale integration \(Simphony shipboard POS\), spa management, shore excursion booking
- Crew time and attendance with ILO Maritime Labour Convention compliance
- Multi\-currency cash management, credit card settlement via Oracle Payment Interface
- Fleet Management System integration for shoreside KPI monitoring and real\-time data transfer
- On\-prem N\-tier architecture running Oracle Database on shipboard servers with Windows infrastructure

This is a globally rare expertise\. The number of people who combine deep operational knowledge of cruise SPMS, 25\+ years of enterprise IT leadership, and a PhD in Business Administration is vanishingly small\. That scarcity is your pricing power\.

# <a id="_Toc224818289"></a>Navigating Oracle’s Outside Employment Policies

Oracle’s Code of Ethics and Business Conduct \(CEBC\) addresses conflicts of interest and outside activities\. The key principles to follow:

## <a id="_Toc224818290"></a>What You Must Do

1. Review your specific employment agreement, offer letter, and any non\-compete, non\-solicitation, or IP assignment clauses\. These override general policy guidance\.
2. If Oracle requires disclosure of outside employment \(most large tech companies do\), file a proactive disclosure for both the FAU adjunct role and the consulting LLC\. Transparency protects you\.
3. Never use Oracle equipment, email, IP, confidential information, or work time for consulting or teaching activities\.
4. Maintain a clear separation: Oracle work stays on Oracle devices during Oracle hours; consulting and teaching use personal devices on personal time\.

## <a id="_Toc224818291"></a>The Critical Boundary: Conflict of Interest

__While employed at Oracle Hospitality, your consulting must not:__

- Advise cruise lines or hospitality companies on Oracle SPMS implementations, configurations, or migrations—that’s Oracle’s business and a direct conflict
- Recommend competing PMS products to Oracle customers or prospects
- Use any Oracle proprietary information, internal documentation, or customer data
- Solicit Oracle employees or customers for your consulting business

__What is cleanly separated and permissible:__

- Vendor\-neutral digital transformation strategy consulting
- IT governance, ITIL, and enterprise architecture advisory \(especially with TOGAF certification\)
- Executive education workshops on general IT operations management topics
- Expert network calls \(GLG, Guidepoint\) sharing general industry knowledge—not Oracle proprietary information
- Teaching graduate courses at FAU on IT management and operations
- Publishing in academic and practitioner journals
- Speaking at industry conferences on general digital transformation topics

*After leaving Oracle: the non\-compete constraint lifts \(subject to any post\-employment restrictive covenants in your agreement\), and you can fully leverage your SPMS and cruise line operational expertise for consulting\. This is when the consulting business takes off\. The side hustle you build now is the infrastructure for that future\.*

# <a id="_Toc224818292"></a>Optimizing the FAU Adjunct Role

The adjunct position at FAU Executive Education is not just a teaching gig—it’s a strategic asset that serves three functions: academic credibility, AACSB SA status, and an institutional platform for your broader brand\.

## <a id="_Toc224818293"></a>Maximizing the Arrangement

1. Maintain current course load\. One or two courses per term in ITOM for MBA, MS Data Science, and MS Business Analytics students fits around Oracle’s schedule and doesn’t trigger performance concerns\.
2. Explore higher\-value teaching formats\. Summer intensives, weekend executive education modules, and corporate training partnerships often pay better per hour than standard adjunct courses and are easier to schedule around a full\-time job\.
3. Propose a new course\. Something like “Digital Transformation in Practice” or “AI Strategy for Operations Leaders” positions you as indispensable and builds intellectual property you can also use in consulting engagements\.
4. Request the strongest possible title\. Some universities offer “Senior Adjunct,” “Adjunct Associate Professor,” or “Affiliate Faculty” designations based on tenure and performance\. A stronger title enhances your brand\.
5. Continue building the teaching portfolio\. Updated syllabi, student evaluations, sample assignments—these are essential if you ever pursue a full\-time professor of practice position\.

## <a id="_Toc224818294"></a>What the Adjunct Role Does for Your Brand

- __AACSB SA Status: __Your PhD \(completed 2024\) provides Scholarly Academic classification through approximately 2029\. This makes you valuable for FAU’s accreditation compliance\.
- __Institutional Affiliation: __Your byline reads “René Bordás, Ph\.D\., Oracle Hospitality & Florida Atlantic University”—that dual identity carries weight in publishing, speaking, and consulting\.
- __Future Option Value: __If a full\-time professor of practice position opens at FAU, FIU, UM, or NSU, you’re the internal candidate with a track record\. That door stays open\.
- __Journal Article Collaboration: __Your work with Chris on converting the dissertation to a journal article is facilitated by the ongoing university relationship\.

# <a id="_Toc224818295"></a>Building the Consulting LLC as a Side Hustle

## <a id="_Toc224818296"></a>Setup \(Month 1\)

1. Register a single\-member LLC through Sunbiz\.org \(Florida Division of Corporations\)—$125 filing fee\. Single\-member LLC taxed as sole proprietor on Schedule C\.
2. Elect S\-corp status \(Form 2553\) once consulting income exceeds $40K–$50K annually\. This saves self\-employment tax on the portion taken as distributions rather than salary\. You’ve already researched this structure\.
3. Open a separate business checking account and obtain an EIN from the IRS \(free, online, instant\)\.
4. Set up simple invoicing \(Wave, FreshBooks, or QuickBooks Self\-Employed\)\.
5. Brand the LLC around vendor\-neutral expertise: digital transformation strategy, IT operations governance, enterprise architecture advisory, and executive education\. Avoid naming Oracle or SPMS in any marketing while employed\.

## <a id="_Toc224818297"></a>Revenue Streams \(Ranked by Speed to First Dollar\)

__Stream 1: Expert Network Calls \(Week 1–2\)__

Register on GLG, Guidepoint, AlphaSights, and Tegus\. These platforms connect you with private equity firms, consulting firms, and investors who pay $300–$800/hour for 30–60 minute calls\. They want your perspective on cruise line IT operations, hospitality technology trends, and enterprise IT governance—general industry expertise, not Oracle proprietary information\. Low time commitment, high value, no conflict with Oracle\.

__Stream 2: Executive Education & Corporate Workshops \(Month 2–3\)__

Deliver workshops on weekends or during PTO\. Topics: “IT Governance Frameworks for Hospitality,” “Digital Transformation Strategy for Operations Leaders,” “Enterprise Architecture Fundamentals\.” Target hospitality industry associations \(HFTP, CLIA, AHLA\) and university executive education programs\. Rate: $5K–$15K per session\.

__Stream 3: Advisory Board Seats \(Month 3–6\)__

Hospitality technology startups and South Florida tech companies value a PhD\-holding enterprise IT leader on their advisory board\. These typically pay $5K–$20K annually in equity or cash, require minimal time \(quarterly meetings plus ad hoc calls\), and expand your network\. Target the Miami fintech and travel\-tech ecosystem\.

__Stream 4: Vendor\-Neutral Consulting Engagements \(Month 6\+\)__

Once the LLC is established and the expert network calls generate referrals, pursue project\-based consulting: IT strategy assessments, enterprise architecture reviews, and digital transformation roadmaps for mid\-market companies\. Rate: $200–$400/hour\. Keep these strictly vendor\-neutral while at Oracle\.

# <a id="_Toc224818299"></a>Publishing in HBR, MIT Sloan, and Cornell Hospitality Quarterly

## <a id="_Toc224818300"></a>Why Bridge Journals Matter for You

Bridge journals sit between pure academic research and trade publications\. They reach practicing executives, earn academic credibility, and build a portable reputation\. For someone with your profile—deep industry expertise plus doctoral research rigor—they are the highest\-impact publication outlet\.

## <a id="_Toc224818301"></a>What HBR Looks For

HBR evaluates submissions on five criteria: expertise, evidence, originality, practical applicability, and accessible writing\. You don’t need to be famous\. HBR routinely publishes academics whose authority comes from careful analysis, study, and experience\. The most common rejection reason is “Our readers already know this”—so the bar is fresh insight, not name recognition\.

HBR understands that practitioner research often deals with novel or emerging issues where large data sets don’t exist\. They want insights grounded in reality and practitioners’ lived experiences, not theory alone\. Your 25 years of enterprise IT leadership provides exactly that foundation\.

### <a id="_Toc224818303"></a>How to Submit to HBR

Submit through HBR’s Submittable portal at harvardbusinessreview\.submittable\.com\. Start with HBR\.org \(digital\), not the print magazine—it publishes more frequently, has a faster editorial cycle, and is a more accessible entry point\. For the print magazine, email proposals to hbr\_editorial@hbr\.org\. Expect 6–8 weeks for a response\.

## <a id="_Toc224818304"></a>Article Concept Development

Your strongest HBR pitch leverages the practitioner\-scholar intersection\. Two concepts:

__Concept A: __“What Running IT on a Cruise Ship Teaches You About Disconnected Operations\.” Cruise ships operate critical IT infrastructure with intermittent satellite connectivity, multi\-jurisdictional regulatory compliance, and a captive user population\. The operational lessons transfer directly to any organization managing edge computing, remote\-site IT, or disaster\-resilient infrastructure\. This is a novel problem that HBR’s audience hasn’t heard before\.

__Concept B: __“The PhD You Don’t Expect: What Happens When a 25\-Year IT Executive Goes Back to School\.” A personal narrative about what academic research revealed that decades of industry experience missed—and vice versa\. This fits HBR’s appetite for counterintuitive insights and personal authority\.
