# Chapter 2 - Literature Review

To set the broader topic of this study in context, a literature review
was performed to outline the boundaries of new venture performance and
mentoring. What follows in this chapter is a review of academic
literature on new venture performance, mentoring of entrepreneurs,
social learning theory, and the effects of human capital.

Organizational performance is an important topic in management research.
Cameron (1978) concluded in his study that the concept varies based on
the body of research, level of analysis, areas of an organization, and
research purpose. Not only is performance multidimensional, but it is
also not just one monolithic concept. Instead, performance represents a
multifaceted construct encompassing multiple domains operationalized
differently. Chakravarty (1986) categorized performance measures into
measures of profitability (e.g., return on investment \[ROI\], return on
sales, growth in revenues, cash flow/investment, market share, market
share gain, product quality relative to competitors, new product
activities relative to competitors, direct cost relative to competitors,
product research and development \[R&D\], process R&D, variations in
ROI, percentage point change in ROI, percentage point change in cash
flow/investment) and financial-market measures (e.g., market-to-book
ratio).

However, many of these accounting and market-based measures are not used
for new ventures due to the lack of deep historical data for young
firms. As new ventures face a higher risk of failure, common criteria
for new venture performance are survival and growth, which encapsulate
the multidimensional aspects of new venture performance. New ventures
encounter the liability of newness as they are exposed to an increased
risk of failure due to a lack of growth (Brüderl et al., 1992; March,
2013). A related concept is the liability of smallness, where firm
survival is at risk when growth is lacking, resulting in a shortage of
critical resources needed to create structures and processes to execute
the strategy (Carroll, 1983; Gimenez-Fernandez et al., 2020). With
lagging growth, new and smaller ventures face increased risks for
survival. However, as ventures grow larger and older, the negative
effects of the liabilities of newness and size diminish (Freeman et al.,
1983; Gilbert et al., 2006).

## Firm Growth

Firm growth is a fundamental area of study in new venture research and
pertains to an expansion over a period of time (Nason & Wiklund, 2018).
One of the main questions in this stream of research is related to the
relatively high rate of variance of new venture growth rates. Why do
some new ventures grow more than others (Gilbert et al., 2006)? Models
of new venture growth often emphasize that entrepreneurs must actively
seek growth. Combined with the availability of resources, strategic
choices, operating in a growth-friendly industry, and development of
growth-supporting structures and processes, firms are in a good position
to grow (Baum et al., 2001; Chrisman et al., 1998; Gilbert et al.,
2006). Two seminal contributions to the growth research are Penrose's
(1959) theory of the growth of the firm and Barney's (1991)
resource-based view (RBV).

Penrose theorized that growth is related to the productive use of excess
resources and the flexibility of resources to be used for other tasks.
When resources possess flexibility, their effective utilization
encourages and enables growth. The flexibility of resources allows the
firm to utilize the resources for purposes that differ from their
intended use. A firm can create new services and offerings resulting
from resource flexibility, providing a basis for growth (Penrose, 1959).
The growth that is based on the use of excess resources combined with
the flexibility of resources aligns with Schumpeter's (1934) work on
entrepreneurial innovation as a basis for economic change and growth.

Barney posited that firms with VRIN resources can achieve sustained
competitive advantages. Economic value is generated by the value (V) and
rarity (R) of resources, whereas economic moats are created by imperfect
inimitable (I) and nonsubstitutable (N) resources (Barney, 1991;
Peteraf, 1993). Firms that possess VRIN resources have the potential to
achieve superior performance, compared to firms lacking VRIN resources
(Peteraf & Barney, 2003).

Birley and Westhead (1990) studied the effect of types of small firms,
internal variables of ownership and external variables of product/market
position on three measures of growth: the number of employees, revenue,
and profitability. The number of employees and revenue were related to
the firm's ownership structure, age, and market position. Results
provided evidence that growing firms develop more complex management
structures. No indication was found for a positive relationship with the
use of outside advisors. Profitability was associated with a widening
gap between owners and management and negatively related to outside
advisors.

Gilbert et al. (2006) literature review explored determinants of new
venture growth disparities. The entrepreneur's traits and access to
human capital and finance resources were identified as important
factors. Gilbert et al. (2006) identified growth measures, including
sales, employment, and market share growth. Sales growth reflects a
change over time and implies customers' growing acceptance of the firms'
products and services. The growing revenues can be reinvested into the
firm to expand resources and capabilities for further growth. An
increase in employment within a firm reflects strategic or compositional
changes (Hanks et al., 1994). Often, this growth corresponds to an
expanded operational scope or an immediate business expansion. By
growing its workforce, firms gain access to new human capital that could
allow for objective execution, more effective competitive analysis, and
sustained competitiveness (Box et al., 1994). Employment growth can also
be viewed as signaling the contribution the venture makes to the
community (Kirchhoff & Phillips, 1988; Venkataraman et al., 1990).
Similar to sales growth, market share growth reflects the market's
positive reception of a firm's offerings. However, market share is an
external metric tied to industry competition. A rise in market share
could also stem from a competitor's exit or the firms' strategic
initiatives (Gilbert et al., 2006; Kerin et al., 1992).

In summary, firm growth is a central topic in new venture research,
examining how ventures expand over time (Nason & Wiklund, 2018). A key
issue in this research area is explaining the considerable variation in
growth rates and scale among new ventures (Gilbert et al., 2006).
Research by Birley and Westhead (1990) and Gilber et al. (2006)
investigates how factors such as ownership structure, human capital, and
market conditions affect growth indicators like sales, employment, and
market share. Penrose's (1959) theory highlights the importance of
utilizing resources flexibly to drive growth, while Barney's (1991)
resource-based view (RBV) identifies the advantage of firms possessing
valuable, rare, imperfectly inimitable, and non-substitutable (VRIN)
resources. Additionally, studies suggest that entrepreneurs need to
actively seek growth opportunities, with success depending on the
availability of resources, strategic decisions, and supportive
environments (Baum et al., 2001; Chrisman et al., 1998). Social
learning theory (Bandura, 1977) provides a complementary lens for
understanding how entrepreneurs acquire the growth-oriented behaviors
and resource strategies described above — by observing experienced
peers, absorbing feedback, and modeling the practices of successful
ventures in their environment.

## Firm Survival

This section examines firm survival as the second key dimension of new
venture performance, addressing the question of what factors determine
whether a new venture can sustain independent operations over time.
Survival is a performance measure that indicates a venture's capacity to
independently operate (Barney, 1986; Chrisman et al., 1998). The
opposite is venture failure, and is reached when the venture can no
longer maintain its status as an economic entity (Barney, 1986), such as
an inability to meet financial obligations (Chrisman et al., 1998).
Stinchcombe's (1965) theory of liability of newness suggests that
nascent organizational forms are at risk of failure unless they address
challenges related to understanding and implementing new structures.
While many new organizational forms fail, successful ones often thrive
by introducing novel approaches to organizing work that persist despite
external changes and competitive pressures (March, 2013).

Brüderl et al. (1992) categorized factors influencing the survival
prospects of new enterprises into four factors, namely individual
characteristics of the founder, attributes of the business, its
structure, and the external environment. The research findings indicate
that founder characteristics, such as industry-specific experience,
self-employment history, leadership abilities, and having a
self-employed father, lowered failure rates. Moreover, business age was
a significant attribute, with newly established businesses facing a
higher risk of failure compared to their more established counterparts.
Size, measured as the amount of capital invested, number of employees,
and legal structure, play a significant role, with smaller businesses
having a survival disadvantage. Significant external environment
characteristics for a higher likelihood of failure included operating in
a highly competitive environment or a highly seasonal business in
wholesale/retail, transportation, and restaurants. Conversely,
businesses operating in industries with high clustering of business
orders (a few but large deals), manufacturing, construction, and
computer services exhibited better survival rates (Brüderl et al.,
1992).

A comprehensive review by Soto-Simeone et al. (2020) explored the
literature on new venture survival. Extending the notion of "liability
of newness" introduced by Stinchcombe (1965), the review identified
three antecedents affecting new ventures. These antecedents encompass
the environmental context, the internal dynamics and choices made by new
ventures, and the individual attributes of founders and founding teams.
In the environmental context, conditions such as task environment,
institutional environment, political environment, and technological
environment were identified in the literature (Carroll, 1987;
Soto-Simeone et al., 2020). The attributes of internal dynamics and
choices made by new ventures include firm age, size (as it relates to
the liability of smallness), and firms' organizational structure. The
third group is the individual characteristics of founders and founding
teams. This stream of research started with Brüderl et al. (1992) and
was based on Becker's (1964) human capital theory. Topics of interest
were the associations between new venture survival and founders'
characteristics, such as years of schooling, work experience, and
industry-specific knowledge (Soto-Simeone et al., 2020). Furthermore,
the authors identified three groups used by researchers to categorize
the survival of new ventures. The first group for categorizing new
venture survival relies on financial performance indicators. These
include events such as bankruptcy or insolvency and are clear and
observable financial outcomes that serve as operational survival
measures. The second approach involves assessing traceability.
Researchers examine whether a firm can still be in the market through
the web, phone, or email. By evaluating firms' responsiveness and
presence, researchers determine whether the venture continues to exist.
The third group defines survival based on stakeholder involvement. For
instance, the discontinuance of ownership by central stakeholders
indicates a venture\'s increased risk of survival. These three groups
provide a comprehensive understanding of new venture survival
considering financial health, market presence, and the dynamics of key
stakeholders (Soto-Simeone et al., 2020). The third group was the least
common approach used by researchers. The authors highlighted that
(dis)continuance is important in shaping a definition of firm survival
but also noted that it does not automatically imply financial success or
failure. Some ventures are "living dead" (Ruhnka et al., 1992), while
others cease operations after successful sales (Wennberg et al., 2010).

In summary, firm survival measures a venture\'s ability to operate
independently over time, compared with venture failure, which results
from a business\'s inability to maintain its economic status (Barney,
1986; Chrisman et al., 1998). According to Stinchcombe's (1965)
liability of newness theory, new ventures face increased risks due to
the difficulties of adapting to new organizational forms, though
successful ventures often overcome these challenges by innovating their
operational structures (March, 2013). Brüderl et al. (1992) identified
that founder attributes, business age, size, and external environmental
factors significantly impact survival, with newer and smaller businesses
generally facing higher risks. Soto-Simeone et al. (2020) expanded on
these findings by identifying three main survival determinants:
environmental conditions, internal dynamics, and founder
characteristics. Outlined methods for assessing survival include
financial health, market presence, and stakeholder involvement, and
indicating that some ventures may persist despite financial issues or
close after achieving success (Ruhnka et al., 1992; Wennberg et al.,
2010). Social learning theory (Bandura, 1977) is particularly relevant
here: many of the founder characteristics that mitigate survival risk —
such as industry-specific experience and leadership ability — are
themselves developed through observational learning and modeling of
experienced entrepreneurs, underscoring the role of social learning
processes in building the competencies that new ventures need to
survive.

The relationship between mentoring of entrepreneurs and firm survival
and growth is pivotal in addressing the challenges identified by
Stinchcombe's (1965) liability of newness and subsequent research.
Mentoring helps entrepreneurs navigate the complexities of
organizational adaptation and external pressures, which is critical for
improving their ventures chances of survival (March, 2013). Research
indicates that entrepreneurs' attributes, business size, and the
environmental conditions affect survival, and mentors can help mitigate
these risks by offering expertise and resources specific to the
entrepreneurs' needs (Brüderl et al., 1992; Soto-Simeone et al., 2020).
Mentoring also supports venture growth by helping entrepreneurs leverage
resources effectively, make strategic decisions, and pursue
opportunities, which aligns with Penrose's (1959) emphasis on flexible
resource utilization and Barney's (1991) resource-based view (RBV) of
competitive advantage (Baum et al., 2001). As such, the mentoring of
entrepreneurs is important for both overcoming survival challenges and
driving venture growth (Nason & Wiklund, 2018).

## Mentoring and Entrepreneurs

This section explores mentoring as the primary intervention mechanism
through which entrepreneurs in this study develop the knowledge and
skills needed to improve new venture performance. It addresses the
question of how mentoring relationships function and what outcomes they
produce for nascent entrepreneurs.
According to scholarly works, the origins of mentorship are traced back
to the realm of ancient Greek mythology. The term "mentor" finds its
roots in Homer's *The Odyssey*. Odysseus entrusts his son Telemachus to
Mentor, who assumes the dual role of educator and identity-shaper. When
Mentor interacts with Telemachus, the wisdom of goddess Anthea flows
through him (Moulson, 2015; St-Jean & Audet, 2013; St-Jean & Tremblay,
2011, 2020). Roberts (2000), however, posited that the term "mentor"
originated in *Les Aventures de T*é*l*é*maque*, by Fénelon (1699), and
the word itself did not become part of common vocabulary until 1750. In
the 18^th^ and 19^th^ centuries, mentorship was closely associated with
introducing young men to military service or trade (Burton et al., 2017;
Campbell et al., 2012).

In the 1970s, mentoring emerged as a significant focus in education and
corporate contexts (Dziczkowski, 2013). Zuckerman's (1977) research
highlighted a link between the success of American laureates and
mentoring. Specifically, over half of the laureates by 1972 had
collaborated with seasoned laureates and other influential non-laureates
(Dziczkowski, 2013; Tenner, 2004). Kram (1983, 1985) expanded upon that
research and developed seminal work on mentorship and two research
streams, namely career-oriented and psychological mentoring.
Career-oriented mentoring encompasses socialization to professional
environments and support through sponsorship, exposure and visibility,
protection, coaching, and challenging work assignments. Psychological
mentoring involves role modeling, validation, psychological support,
counseling, and friendship. Furthermore, mentoring enhances
introspective abilities, coping skills, and openness to new experiences
(Campbell et al., 2012; Moulson, 2015; Scandura, 1992; Wilbanks, 2013).
However, Scandura (1992) posited that role modeling should be recognized
as an independent third function within mentoring, distinct from
career-oriented and psychological mentoring (Wilbanks, 2013).

There are over 50 definitions of mentoring that are dependent on the
context (Crisp & Cruz, 2009; Iyamu & Isizah, 2021). Mentoring is a
social learning process rooted in social interaction between mentor
and mentee (Bandura, 1977; Crisp & Cruz, 2009). A mentor, often possessing specific
qualities or holding a position of authority, actively guides a younger,
less experienced individual (the mentee or protégé). Through this
relationship, the mentee gains valuable support, visibility, resources,
and direction (Betts & Pepe, 2006; Burke, 1984; Joiner et al., 2004;
Kram, 1985; Ragins, 1989). Through these functions, both the mentor and
mentee and their organizations gain benefits (Betts & Pepe, 2006; Joiner
et al., 2004).

Although often used interchangeably, coaching and mentoring are distinct
forms of professional development that serve different purposes (D'Abate
et al., 2003; Davies & Taylor, 2004; Haggard et al., 2011). On the one
hand, coaching is a more structured, short-term process with the
objective of improving specific skills and performance through goal
setting and feedback, typically involving a business relationship with
financial compensation (D'Abate et al., 2003; McKevitt & Marshall,
2015). On the other hand, mentoring is characterized by a long-term,
informal relationship focused on broad personal and professional growth,
including guidance and support rather than targeted skill development
(Deschamps et al., 2010; St-Jean, 2012). It often involves a mentor who
provides overall guidance while mentees set their own developmental
goals (Audet & Couteret, 2012; Bisk, 2002; Thompson & Downing, 2007).
Despite their differences, coaching can be integrated into mentoring
relationships to address specific performance issues and skill
development (Klofsten & Öberg, 2012; Utrilla & Torraleja, 2013).

In the entrepreneurial context, although other definitions are possible,
mentoring is a support relationship between a novice entrepreneur
(mentee) and an experienced entrepreneur (mentor), where the latter
helps the former develop as a person (St-Jean & Audet, 2013; St-Jean &
Tremblay, 2011). In this relationship, innovative thinking and
risk-taking are developed through three general functions: vocational
support (coaching), psychological support (encouraging), and role
modeling (demonstrating) (Godshalk & Sosik, 2000; Moore & Wang, 2017;
Scandura & Ragins, 1993). Furthermore, novice entrepreneurs, guided by
mentors, identify emerging opportunities, improve their management
expertise, and formulate a vision for their new business (Moulson, 2015;
Ozgen & Baron, 2007; St-Jean, 2012).

Deakins et al. (1998) examined the literature on startup business
assistance. They observed that such support often contradicts much of
the prevalent scholarly opinion which criticizes startup support through
public sector assistance programs. Instead, the scholarly opinion was to
encourage providing support to existing, more mature firms instead of to
new ventures. The results of the first stage of Deakins et al.'s study
indicated that projected performance figures of the startups were
notably high and a perceived high value of mentorship by the new
entrepreneurs, countering the prevalent scholarly opinion.

St-Jean (2012) posited that mentoring can help overcome traditional
barriers to managerial training for small- and medium-sized enterprises.
The barriers include diverse and unique basic training needs because of
the firm's small size, which are challenging to address with a course
curriculum developed for widespread use (Coetzer et al., 2011; Gibb,
1997; Mangham & Silver, 1986). Customized training courses for new
ventures can be costly to create and conduct, and their outcomes can be
challenging to measure (Wheeless & Grotz, 1977). The heterogeneity of
entrepreneurs' backgrounds, needs, and ambitions indicates that a
'one-size-fits-all' approach to training is neither cost-effective nor
time-efficient (Fuller‐Love, 2006; Hessels et al., 2008; Smith & Miner,
1983). Moreover, these traditional barriers may contribute to
Stinchcombe's (1965) liability of newness, as 'one-size-fits-all'
training designed for more established firms is insufficient for new
entrepreneurs who need to develop innovative ways to organize their
businesses to help mitigate external pressures and changes.

Due to the different needs and requirements of the self-employed
compared to business managers, mentorship for entrepreneurs differs from
conventional workplace mentoring (Brodie et al., 2017; Waters et al.,
2002). Traditional workplace mentoring typically involves the mentor,
usually someone with a higher rank, defining goals for the mentee.
Whereas in an entrepreneur mentoring relationship, the mentee drives the
goal setting, and both mentor and mentee contribute unique expertise to
the relationship (Davies & Taylor, 2004; Gold et al., 2003). As a
result, in the entrepreneurial context, the relationship between mentor
and mentee is equal and more flexible, but less structured and
rule-driven than traditional workplace mentoring relationships (Brodie
et al., 2017; Davies & Taylor, 2004).

Studies have highlighted the positive cognitive impacts of mentoring of
entrepreneurs, such as opportunity recognition, increased self-efficacy,
the refinement of business strategies, and the development of management
skills (Ozgen & Baron, 2007; St-Jean & Audet, 2012). Additionally,
studies have shown positive emotional learning effects, such as reduced
feelings of doubt and isolation, improved self-efficacy, and the
establishment of more challenging goals (St-Jean & Tremblay, 2020).
Through mentoring, the mentee can learn important business skills, such
as business plan development and managing finances. Furthermore, the
entrepreneur learns interpersonal and emotional competencies that are
important for new entrepreneurs and other soft skills, such as
self-efficacy and confidence (Brodie et al., 2017; St-Jean & Audet,
2012). The relationship with a mentor gives the entrepreneur
opportunities to gain emotional support, learn the decision-making
process, build a professional image, and connect with the entrepreneur
community (Radu Lefebvre & Redien-Collot, 2013; St-Jean & Audet, 2012;
Terjesen & Sullivan, 2011). Consequently, entrepreneurs improve their
well-being, independence, personal satisfaction, and purpose to continue
their entrepreneurial journey. The support and advice provided through
the relationship, both formal and informal, can lead to the
entrepreneur's improved working conditions, interaction with employees,
and decision-making (Baluku et al., 2020).

In summary, mentorship, although deeply rooted in ancient Greek
mythology where the term was first used in Homer's "*The Odyssey*" to
describe the guidance provided to Telemachus (Moulson, 2015; St-Jean &
Audet, 2013), initially centered around military and trade training
during the 18th and 19th centuries (Burton et al., 2017; Campbell et
al., 2012). In the 1970s, the scope of mentorship broadened to encompass
educational and corporate settings, as highlighted by research from
Zuckerman (1977) and Kram (1983; 1985), which underscored its
significance in career and psychological development. In the
entrepreneurial sphere, mentorship is pivotal, offering vocational
coaching, psychological support, and role modeling, which helps
entrepreneurs in identifying opportunities and managing their businesses
effectively (Godshalk & Sosik, 2000; St-Jean & Audet, 2013). Thus,
mentorship is instrumental in addressing barriers to managerial training
and contributes to better business strategies, emotional resilience, and
overall success in entrepreneurship (Brodie et al., 2017; St-Jean,
2012).

## Social Learning Theory

This section presents social learning theory as the primary theoretical
framework for this study, addressing the question of how observational
learning and modeling explain the process through which entrepreneur
mentees acquire the behaviors and strategies that drive new venture
performance. Bandura's (1977) social learning theory asserts that individuals learn
by observing others, receiving feedback, and modeling behaviors they
have observed. This theory emphasizes that learning involves a
continuous, reciprocal interaction among cognitive, behavioral, and
environmental factors, as individuals imitate the behaviors, attitudes,
and emotional responses of others.

Mentoring is a social learning approach characterized by a relationship
between a mentor, who has more experience or knowledge, and a mentee,
who has less. This interaction allows the mentee, particularly
entrepreneurs, to acquire new information and address their lack of
experiences (Ucbasaran et al., 2009). Through interactions with the
entrepreneur, the mentor shares their experience, enabling the
entrepreneur to broaden their cognitive framework and potentially
improve their capacity for recognizing opportunities (St-Jean &
Tremblay, 2011).

Observational learning enables the acquisition and integration of
knowledge through four key processes: attention, retention,
reproduction, and motivation. The attentional process determines what
aspects of what is being modeled are focused on and what is learned from
these observations (Bandura, 1977). Retention relates to the storage of
observed behaviors in memory, allowing individuals to recall and apply
these behaviors even without the presence of the model (Bandura, 1977).
The motor reproduction process focuses on converting observed behaviors
into actual actions (Bandura, 1977). Motivational process influences
whether the observer will implement the observed behaviors. The theory
distinguishes between the acquisition and performance, as observers do
not always perform all the behaviors they have observed (Bandura, 1977).

Learning in observers is influenced by the effectiveness of behaviors
demonstrated by the role model. Observing positive outcomes from
successful behaviors can boost the observer's self-efficacy and
motivation, encouraging them to adopt and apply new knowledge and
behaviors (Okorley et al., 2024). According to Bandura (1977),
self-efficacy is defined as the belief in one's own capability to
execute the necessary actions to achieve specific results. Increased
self-efficacy reduces fear and hesitation while boosting expectations of
success, which in turn affects the observer's coping strategies once
they begin (Bandura, 1977). Anticipating success acts as a motivator and
confidence enhancer, thereby affecting the observer's perception of
their own abilities to achieve desired outcomes (Audia et al., 2000;
Bandura, 1977; Okorley et al., 2024).

Feedback from the role model assists the observer to advance their
existing knowledge and skills and identify areas for improvement, which
allows for adjustments in their practices. Understanding whether one's
performance is correct or incorrect can improve and maintain behavior
over an extended period. Feedback helps by detailing the types of
mistakes made and how they might be addressed. Even when the feedback is
only information about the level of performance without specific
corrective guidance, it can enhance performance. In such cases,
informative feedback primarily functions as a motivator rather than as a
tool for correcting responses (Bandura, 1977).

When the entrepreneur adopts the observed behavior, they apply and
assimilate the new knowledge within their venture's particular context.
They can then evaluate if these behaviors achieve the desired results.
Ongoing mentorship allows the entrepreneur to receive continuous
feedback and discuss any necessary corrections. Thus, the mentoring
relationship persists and becomes increasingly focused on the specifics
of the venture.

Within the study's context, social learning frames the mentoring
process, where new entrepreneurs learn by observing experienced mentors.
The mentee benefits from observing the mentor's successful business
practices, interactions with various stakeholders, and strategies for
handling challenges and opportunities. The relationship allows the
mentee to ask questions, seek guidance, and receive feedback, while the
mentor offers support and constructive advice. For the mentoring process
to be effective, the mentee must apply the acquired knowledge to their
venture, experiment with the mentor's suggestions, and assess their
outcomes. This iterative approach helps the mentee acquire valuable
skills and insights, contributing to the venture's long-term competitive
advantage and mitigating risks associated with being new or small.

In summary, social learning theory (Bandura, 1977) serves as the
integrating framework across this literature review, explaining how
observational learning, feedback, and role modeling connect
entrepreneurial mentoring, firm survival, and new venture growth into a
coherent theoretical foundation for this study. The four
constructs — firm growth, firm survival, mentoring, and social learning
theory — are not independent topics but a unified explanatory chain:
entrepreneurs learn by observing experienced mentors (SLT), which
develops the competencies that drive growth and survival outcomes in new
ventures.
